Blog of a home crypto miner 2 months post Ethereum mining

I have been following the cryptocurrency markets for a long time, going back to my high school days in the early 2010s.

In 2020, during the Covid-19 Pandemic, I became interested in cryptocurrency mining after getting a stock tip about a cryptocurrency mining stock from my uncle. After some research, I found out that crypto mining stocks like MARA and RIOT were skyrocketing, along with the rest of the crypto market.

Googling, “how to mine Bitcoin on Windows 10,” lead me to a piece of mining software called NiceHash. I started mining on a single gaming PC that I built from old parts with NiceHash software in the background. NiceHash was running both PhoenixMiner and XMRig mining Ethereum and Monero respectively for the people renting my hash power. I got some Bitcoin in exchange as payment from NiceHash. The hash power renter paid NiceHash Bitcoin for my hash power and some of that was shared with me.

After Ethereum went proof of stake, most of the mining profits dried up. This is because Ethereum was paying out a vast majority of the daily payments to miners prior to the merge.

Currently, instead of miners getting paid newly minted Ethereum for processing transactions, stakers only get paid for running nodes they received the privilege of running by staking 32 Ethereum. Miners are left to mine other coins, now less profitably.

At the height of the mining market, I was renting out my Ethereum and Monero hash power for Bitcoin and earning over $30 a day with only three gaming computers. The GPUs were mining Ethereum, and the CPUs were mining Monero. Later, I brought on an older GPU with less memory, with which I rented out Ravencoin hash power.

Along the way, I added three Application Specific Integrated Circuits (ASICs) for mining. Then I brought the old GPU offline along with another older GPU and added a new GPU and three new computers. This modernized my farm of PCs and I am now running some of the most current hardware.

I am currently mining Neoxa on my GPUs and Monero on my CPUs. I have one ASIC mining Grin, and two ASICs mining Ethereum Classic. I have developed a website to monitor my mining stats in real time from the 2Miners Mining Pool. This Mining Pool is a resource for miners to pool their hash power together to get shared frequent payments instead of individual infrequent payments. The website that I've created to monitor my mining stats is www.BlastMining.net.

Neoxa is a new Proof-of-Work and Proof-of-Game cryptocurrency where miners are rewarded for processing transactions, and gamers are rewarded for playing on Neoxa game servers.

Monero and Grin are privacy coins with lots of built in security features.

Ethereum Classic is a Proof-of-Work implementation of Ethereum with a slightly modified algorithm called ETCHash.

Currently, Kaspa looks like an interesting new cryptocurrency to me. It has gotten very popular with miners, and you can even rent out your Kaspa hash power for Bitcoin on NiceHash. I don't think I'll be mining Kaspa on my GPUs, though, but I do plan on buying a bit of it. I don't plan on mining any Kaspa on my GPUs because there are already Fully Programmable Gate Arrays (FPGAs) mining Kaspa with teamredminer and I don't have any FPGAs.

I do have GPUs that Neoxa are perfect for because Neoxa's algorithm is built for GPUs. It rotates algorithms while mining to keep ASICs and FPGAs off the network. Ravencoin pioneered that algorithm (kawpow) and Neoxa forked it.

I look forward to continuing mining in the future and new projects that arise.
